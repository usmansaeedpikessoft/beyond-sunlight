07 Evolutionary Relations



































Chemosynthetic Bacteria: Sulfoxide bacteria

Life on Thalassokhimia originated in the deep ocean around volcanic vents, where bacteria thrived through chemosynthesis—a process in which they convert chemicals released by volcanic eruptions, such as sulfur compounds, into energy. These bacteria established the foundation of the planet's ecosystem. Over millions of years, some organisms evolved to directly consume these chemosynthetic bacteria, developing in close proximity to the vents and relying on the energy-rich chemicals for their survival. As the ecosystem grew more complex, new organisms emerged that could prey on other life forms, leading to the development of a food chain where larger predators hunted smaller organisms that fed on the bacteria.

Near volcanic vents, a type of bacteria called Sulfoxide bacteria evolved. These bacteria thrive by using sulfur and hydrogen compounds from volcanic eruptions. They convert these chemicals into energy through chemosynt.

Primary Consumers: Vento Crawlers

Vento Crawlers are small, worm-like organisms that feed on Sulfoxide bacteria. As primary consumers, they absorb the energy stored in these bacteria, allowing them to grow in size and complexity over time, which, in turn, supports predators like Abyss Hawks.

Predators: Abyss Hawks

Abyss Hawks are larger predatory organisms that prey on Vento crawlers. These predators dominate deep-sea ecosystems, relying entirely on the energy transferred through chemosynthesis at volcanic vents.

Chemosynthesis in Deep Ocean: Solvibacter

Billions of years into the future, the heat generated by underwater volcanoes decreases by 30-40%, leading to a reduction in the intensity of chemosynthesis and prompting organisms to adapt further. As volcanic activity cools, some species evolve to inhabit islands or the ocean's surface, seeking the diminishing light from the red dwarf star. These organisms develop dual energy systems, allowing them to utilize both chemosynthesis and photosynthesis depending on environmental conditions. One such organism, Solvibacter, is a versatile bacterium capable of both processes. When sunlight is available, it shifts to photosynthesis, using light to convert carbon dioxide into energy. In the absence of light, it relies on hydrogen sulfide from nearby hydrothermal vents to perform chemosynthesis.

Producres: Bivalvana Bloom

Bivalvana Bloom is a large, flexible, algae-like organism that floats near the water's surface in the equatorial zone. When exposed to sunlight, it engages in photosynthesis, producing oxygen and organic matter. During periods of darkness, it switches to utilizing sulfur compounds through chemosynthesis, storing energy for later use. As a key producer in this mixed energy-based ecosystem, Bivalvana Bloom provides essential energy for a diverse range of herbivores.

Primary Consumer: Shifting Manta

Shifting Manta is a ray-like creature that feeds on Bivalvana Bloom and Solvibacter. Equipped with specialized gill structures, it effectively filters both phytoplankton and chemotrophic bacteria from the water. Its metabolism can slow during dark periods when food availability decreases, allowing it to conserve energy for leaner times. As a primary consumer, the Shifting Manta feeds on microbial and plant-based life forms, serving as prey for secondary consumers in the ecosystem.

Secondary Consumers: Sulfurfin

Sulfurfin is a sleek predator resembling a large eel, capable of swiftly navigating through the water. It has adapted to hunt Shifting Mantas and other primary consumers in both light and darkness. Utilizing echolocation in low-light and dark conditions, it effectively locates its prey. As the apex predator of the equatorial region, the Sulfurfin plays a crucial role in maintaining the balance of the primary consumer population.

Additionally, some organisms evolved to move toward the ocean's surface following a sudden tectonic shift caused by an underwater earthquake, which disrupted the delicate balance of the volcanic ecosystem. Chemosynthetic organisms such as Vento crawlers, once thriving near volcanic vents, migrated to capture the dim light from the red dwarf star. Predators like Abyss hawks followed their prey into mid-ocean regions in search of food. Surface-dwelling species developed pigmentation and structures to effectively capture and process the weak light. These species adapted to survive with limited energy by utilizing slow photosynthesis and evolving longer lifespans to compensate for the slower energy conversion rate.

RoboForm Bacteria

RoboForm Bacteria thrive in the upper ocean, relying on the faint light emitted by the red dwarf star. This unique type of bacteria has evolved to perform photosynthesis, transforming red light into energy. As a form of microbial life, RoboForm bacteria serve as the planet's primary photosynthesizers, converting light energy into food and sustaining the marine ecosystem.

Ludmilla's

Ludmilla's are simple plant-like organisms that evolved from RoboForm bacteria. Floating near the surface, these organisms absorb as much red light as possible to support their growth. They possess specialized cells that efficiently capture light in low-light environments, giving them a soft luminescent sheen. As descendants of early photosynthetic bacteria, Ludmilla's serve as primary producers in the oceanic food chain.

Sail Gliders

Sail Gliders are small, fish-like creatures known for their ability to glide effortlessly through the water. Their fins enable them to cover large distances while feeding on Ludmillas. With elongated bodies, they exhibit swift and agile movements that help them evade predators. Typically pale blue with shimmering undertones, their coloration aids in blending with their surroundings. As herbivores, Sail Gliders have adapted to graze on the surface layer where photosynthesis is most active, making them a part of the marine vertebrate group that thrives on plant-like organisms.

Sky Talons

Sky Talons are agile predatory organisms that feed on Sail Gliders, completing the photosynthetic food chain at the ocean's surface. Equipped with sharp, retractable fins, they can maneuver quickly through the water while hunting. Their dark, metallic-blue skin provides effective camouflage in deeper waters, and bioluminescent streaks along their sides flash to communicate or intimidate prey. With razor-sharp teeth and large, luminous eyes adapted for low-light vision, Sky Talons belong to a reptilian-like group that has evolved to become the apex predators of Thalassokhimia's oceanic food chain.
