04 Division of the Planet





North Pole Ocean

The Hydrochemica Frigus Ocean covers the North Pole and has a frozen surface with cold temperatures from the top going all the way to the middle layers. These temperatures are as low as -10°C to -2°C As you go deeper, the temperatures rise till 100°C. This ocean is rich in chemicals, and the ice on top traps helium and hydrogen gases from the atmosphere. The name "Hydrochemica Frigus" is Latin for "cold water chemicals," highlighting its liquid state and chemical richness. This ocean is important for the planet’s chemical processes and gas storage.

South Pole Ocean

The Nebulium Abyss Ocean covers the South Pole and features some volcanoes, seamounts, guyots, and vents. This ocean rarely freezes, and if it does, only a thin layer of ice a few centimeters thick forms. The salinity is lower than that of typical Earth oceans due to limited evaporation and the dense and thick atmosphere. Surface temperatures range from -1°C to 5°C but become warmer as you approach the seabed. The name "Nebulium Abyss" reflects its characteristics: "Nebula" refers to clouds, relating to the dense atmosphere, while "abyss" signifies depth. This ocean is rich in minerals and is home to diverse coral reefs, which thrive near the ocean floor. The combination of its unique temperature gradient and mineral richness creates an important habitat for marine life.

Equatorial Ocean

The Calor Ocean, located near the equator, has the highest temperatures on the planet and the most volcanoes, guyots, vents, cold seeps, seamounts, and islands. Most mid-ocean ridges can be found here, making it very geologically active. This ocean has the second highest chemical concentration due to its extreme temperatures and large size. Surface temperatures reach about 100°C in winter and 150-170°C during summer. As you go deeper, temperatures drop to 50-70°C in the middle layers but rise again at the seabed, where they can exceed 180°C. The ocean also has the highest salinity of all oceans. Remarkably, about 70 percent of life on the planet thrives in this ocean, adapting to its harsh conditions. The name "Calor Ocean" comes from the Latin word for "heat," highlighting its high temperatures, especially near the equator.

Titan

The only moon known to have a dense atmosphere other than Earth, Titan is a home away from home (just a few hundred degrees colder!). As a bonus, you get striking views of the Rings of Saturn.

Carina

The Carina Nebula itself was first observed from South Africa’s Cape of Good Hope. Among the nebula’s many nicknames includes the Eta Argus Nebula, which references structures that English astronomer James Dunlop and English astronomer John Herschel likened to the ship of the ancient Greek mythological hero Jason and his team of argonauts.
