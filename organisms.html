06 ORGANISMS





























Northern Chemotrophs: The Basalt Spires


These organisms inhabit the deep, icy oceans near the planet's north pole, forming towering, columnar structures that resemble black rocky forests. They extract energy from sulfur and methane compounds originating from the planet's mantle and are covered in protective mineral layers that provide resistance to pressure and temperature extremes. Thriving in high-pressure, high-temperature environments, they can be found on the ocean floor, extending toward the underside of the ice crust. Their highly specialized root systems enable efficient energy extraction, allowing them to endure extreme conditions despite the absence of sunlight.

Microbial Communities:


Crybobecteria is a psychrophilic (cold-loving) bacterium that flourishes near hydrothermal vents. It utilizes hydrogen sulfide released from these vents to generate energy through chemosynthesis, enabling it to endure extreme cold and high pressure. By forming a microbial mat around the vents, Crybobecteria serves as the primary energy source for the surrounding ecosystem.

Glacial Fern:

The glacial fern is a resilient, root-like organism that thrives around hydrothermal vents. It does not require sunlight; instead, it absorbs minerals from the vent water and converts them into energy through chemosynthesis. As a primary producer, it sustains the ecosystem by releasing energy-rich compounds that serve as food for primary consumers.

Southern Chemotrophs: The Abyssal Webs:

Delicate, web-like organisms spread across the ocean floor, anchored to the rocky substrate by mineral-coated tendrils. They extract chemical energy from hydrogen and iron compounds, supporting an ecosystem that relies entirely on this chemical energy. Thriving in complete darkness, without any sunlight, these organisms can be found in ocean sediments and on the rocky ocean floor. They also support dependent species that feed on their waste products.

Microbial Communities:

Methanocles are methane-utilizing archaea that flourish in the cold methane seeps near the southern pole. They convert methane into organic compounds through chemosynthesis, forming the foundation for life in these areas and playing a crucial role for all other species in the region.

Hydro Stalk

Hydro Stalk is a tubeworm-like chemosynthetic producer that thrives around methane seeps. It extracts hydrogen sulfide from the seep water to sustain itself, relying on symbiotic bacteria within its tissues. As a primary producer, it converts methane and sulfides into energy, supporting an ecosystem that is based on chemical energy.

Seep Grazers:

This small, armored snail feeds on Hydro Stalk. Its shell offers protection from predators, while its radula enables it to scrape algae and other microbial growth from surfaces near methane seeps. As a primary consumer, it grazes on chemosynthetic producers, playing a crucial role in the polar food web.

Living in both the North and South: Giant Tube Worms

Giant Tube Worms (Riftia pachyptila) inhabit deep-sea hydrothermal vents, where they rely on chemosynthetic bacteria housed within a specialized organ called the trophosome. Living in both the North and South: Vent Mussels

Vent Mussels (Bathymodiolus species) are found at hydrothermal vents and cold seeps, where they depend on symbiotic relationships with chemosynthetic bacteria that inhabit their gills. These bacteria utilize hydrogen sulfide or methane to produce organic compounds, providing essential nutrients to the mussels. In this mutualistic relationship, the mussels benefit from the chemical energy available in the vent or seep fluids, which the bacteria harness for their energy needs.

Living in both the North and South: Vent Clams

Vent Clams (Calyptogena species) are found at hydrothermal vents and cold seeps, where they, like mussels, host chemosynthetic bacteria in their gills. These bacteria oxidize hydrogen sulfide or methane, providing essential energy for the clams. In this symbiotic relationship, the clams obtain nutrients from the bacteria, while the bacteria benefit from the habitat provided by the clams and access to the chemicals necessary for chemosynthesis.

Living in both the North and South: Yeti Crab

Yeti Crab (Kiwa hirsuta) resides near hydrothermal vents, where it cultivates chemosynthetic bacteria on its hairy claws, using them as a food source. These bacteria oxidize hydrogen sulfide from the vent fluids. In this symbiotic relationship, the crabs "farm" the bacteria by waving their claws in nutrient-rich vent fluid, allowing the bacteria to thrive. The primary energy source for both the crabs and the bacteria is hydrogen sulfide.
