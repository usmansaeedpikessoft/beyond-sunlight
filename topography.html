05 TOPOGRAPHY








The continental shelf covers 3% of the planet's surface, consisting of shallow waters. The continental slope accounts for just 1%, reflecting the presence of many small islands rather than large landmasses. Below this, the continental rise makes up 2%, aiding in sediment buildup and movement to deeper areas. The numerous islands, covering 15% of the surface, create diverse topography in the planet's predominantly oceanic environment. Icebergs and ice shelves, primarily in polar regions, cover around 5% of the surface, influencing ocean currents and providing habitats for cold-adapted species.

Abyssal plains, covering 30% of the deep ocean floor, are flat regions that offer stable habitats for organisms relying on passive chemosynthesis, and occasionally active chemosynthesis, originating from shallower waters. Ocean trenches, which make up 4% of the ocean floor, create deep zones where hydrothermal vents are found, supporting diverse ecosystems by providing habitats and food sources. Seamounts account for 10% of the ocean floor, adding complexity to marine habitats and supporting a wide range of marine species.

Mid-ocean ridges, covering 15% of the ocean floor, are key areas of tectonic activity. These ridges host hydrothermal vents that emit warm, mineral-rich water, supporting life through chemosynthesis. This process is essential for sustaining marine ecosystems, particularly in the deep ocean.

Guyots, which occupy 2% of the ocean floor, contribute to biodiversity by offering specialized habitats for marine life dependent on passive chemosynthesis. These flat-topped seamounts create unique ecological zones, enriching the overall diversity of the ocean environment.

Coral reefs, though limited to 2% of the surface area, flourish in warm, shallow waters. They play a vital role in maintaining marine biodiversity, providing shelter and food for a wide range of species. Despite their small coverage, reefs are crucial for the health of ocean ecosystems.

Hydrothermal vents and cold seeps each account for 20% of the ocean floor and are vital for supporting life in extreme environments. Cold seeps release methane and hydrogen sulfide, providing energy for communities that thrive under harsh conditions.

There are three types of vents similar to those found on Earth. White smoker vents are predominantly located in the Calor Ocean (Equatorial Ocean), mirroring their distribution near Earth's equator. Black smoker vents are primarily found in the Hydrochemica Frigus (North Pole Ocean), though they also occur in the equatorial regions of the Calor Ocean. Diffuse vents are present in both the Hydrochemica Frigus and the Nebulium Abyss (South Pole Ocean).

Carina

The Carina Nebula itself was first observed from South Africa’s Cape of Good Hope. Among the nebula’s many nicknames includes the Eta Argus Nebula, which references structures that English astronomer James Dunlop and English astronomer John Herschel likened to the ship of the ancient Greek mythological hero Jason and his team of argonauts.
