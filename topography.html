04 TOPOGRAPHY

Details

The continental shelf makes up 3% of the surface area. It consists of shallow waters. The continental slope is only 1%, reflecting the planet's many small islands instead of large landmasses. Below this, the continental rise takes up 2%, helping with sediment buildup and flow to deeper areas. The many islands (15%) scattered across the planet create a variety of topography in the largely oceanic environment. Icebergs and ice shelves also cover about 5% of the planet's surface, mostly in polar regions. They influence ocean currents and provide homes for cold-adapted species.

Details

In the deep ocean, abyssal plains cover 30% of the ocean floor. These flat regions provide stable habitats for organisms that thrive on passive chemosynthesis or in rare cases active as well, from shallower waters. Ocean trenches occupy 4% of the ocean floor and create deep areas where some hydrothermal vents are found. This enhances biodiversity by providing homes and food for organisms. Seamounts cover 10% of the ocean floor and add variety to marine habitats, allowing different marine species to thrive.

Details

Mid-ocean ridges make up 15% of the ocean floor and are sites of tectonic activity. They host hydrothermal vents that release warm, mineral-rich water, which supports life through chemosynthesis. This feature is crucial for sustaining life on the planet. Guyots, at 2%, further enhance this diversity by providing unique homes for specific marine life that is passive chemosynthetic. Coral reefs, limited to 2% of the surface area, thrive in warm, shallow waters and help maintain biodiversity.

Details

Hydrothermal vents and cold seeps each make up 20% of the ocean floor. These areas are essential for life. Cold seeps release methane and hydrogen sulfide which supports communities that thrive in harsh conditions. There are three types of vents in the overall oceans, similar to those on Earth. The white smoker vents are primarily found in the Calor Ocean (Equatorial Ocean), resembling their distribution near the equator on Earth. The black smoker vents are mainly located in the Hydrochemica Frigus (North Pole Ocean) but will also be present in the equatorial regions of the Calor Ocean. Lastly, the diffuse vents are found in both the Hydrochemica Frigus and Nebulium Abyss (South Pole Ocean).

Carina

The Carina Nebula itself was first observed from South Africa’s Cape of Good Hope. Among the nebula’s many nicknames includes the Eta Argus Nebula, which references structures that English astronomer James Dunlop and English astronomer John Herschel likened to the ship of the ancient Greek mythological hero Jason and his team of argonauts.
